# Talk Brainstorm

## Core Thesis

The talk has four points that build on each other:

### 1. The junior developer job is disappearing

Getting hired as a junior computer programmer is harder now than maybe ever before. AI can do the work that junior devs used to do. Companies aren't hiring people to write boilerplate, fix simple bugs, or do the grunt work that used to be the entry ramp into the industry. The bottom rungs of the ladder are being sawed off.

### 2. The top of the ladder has never been more valuable

The rewards for higher-end programming jobs are better than they've ever been in history -- precisely BECAUSE nobody is hiring junior developers any more. The people who can architect systems, think critically, debug novel problems, and build things that don't exist yet are worth more than ever. The gap between "can code" and "can build" has never been wider or more lucrative.

### 3. What separates you is gumption, not skill

The difference between those who make it and those who don't is no longer knowledge or technical skill -- AI levels that playing field. What separates you is:
- **Serious curiosity** -- wanting to know how things work for the sake of knowing, not for the sake of a job
- **Gumption** -- the drive to dig in, figure it out, not give up
- **Follow-through** -- starting AND finishing things
- **Passion that's self-evident** -- not "I'm passionate about technology" on a resume, but a trail of projects, experiments, and rabbit holes that prove it

### 4. Use college strategically (if you go)

College is not where you learn computer science. It's where you:
- **Explore your curiosity broadly** -- study philosophy, history, music, whatever pulls you. The best engineers are well-rounded thinkers.
- **Build your network** -- it's about who you know, not what you know, not the degree you come out with on the other side. Think strategically about this.
- **Follow through on things that interest you** -- the projects and relationships you build in college matter more than the transcript.

This is NOT "don't go to college." This is "if you go, use it deliberately."

## How This Thesis Connects to Each Audience

### Intro to CS (CHS + SCHS Period 4)
These are beginners deciding if CS is for them. The thesis tells them:
- Don't learn to code because you want a job. Learn because you're curious.
- The students in this room who tinker at home, who break things and fix them, who can't stop asking "but how does it work?" -- those are the ones who will thrive.
- The entry-level job that used to be waiting for you won't be there. But if you're genuinely curious, you'll build things that create your own opportunities.

**Saied's note:** Strong gaming interest in this group. Expect questions about game development, AI in games, and how coding connects to that world. Emphasize the value of learning coding skills in HS even as AI advances -- understanding code helps you understand and influence the technology itself.

### Mixed CSP/CSA (SCHS Period 5)
These are more advanced students, some aiming for CS careers. The thesis tells them:
- You're already past the starting line. But the junior dev ramp you're expecting after college may not exist.
- Double down on curiosity and depth. Don't just learn Java syntax -- understand WHY things work the way they do.
- College strategy matters: build relationships, explore broadly, follow through on side projects. The network you build matters more than the GPA.

**Saied's note:** He wants to ask questions about college experience, career path, internships, entrepreneurship, scholarships, and how you chose which areas of CS/AI to pursue. Leave room for this teacher-led Q&A -- practical "how do I actually get there" guidance is what they want most.

## What Students Are Worried About (2025-2026)

- **"Will AI take my job?"** -- 3 in 5 rising seniors feel pessimistic about starting their career
- **"Is college worth it?"** -- growing skepticism about whether education prepares them for reality
- **"Is CS for me?"** -- especially for students who don't fit the stereotype

The thesis directly addresses all three of these fears. It doesn't sugarcoat #1 (yes, the junior market is brutal). It reframes #2 (college is worth it if you use it strategically). And it answers #3 (CS is for you if you're genuinely curious, regardless of your background).

## What Engages High School Students

1. **Live demos** -- build something in real time, even if it breaks (especially if it breaks)
2. **Personal stories** -- journey, not resume; struggles, not just wins
3. **Interactivity** -- Q&A woven throughout, not just at the end; polls; anonymous questions
4. **Connection to their world** -- games, music, social media, AI tools they already use
5. **Respect their intelligence** -- no dumbing down, no forced slang
6. **Keep talking segments under 15-20 minutes** -- then break for activity/discussion

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

- Assuming too much technical knowledge (don't say "API" without explaining it)
- Not waiting long enough after asking questions (push through the silence)
- Talking about yourself without connecting it to them (use "you" more than "I")
- Text-heavy slides or reading from slides
- Making it a monologue
- Using slang to seem cool (backfires every time)

## Talk Structure (Draft)

### Opening (5 min)
Hook -- something unexpected. Maybe: "Raise your hand if you've used ChatGPT this week." (most hands go up) "Okay. Every one of you just did something that would have cost a company $200/hour five years ago. That's what I want to talk about today."

### Part 1: The Honest Truth (10-15 min)
Point #1 and #2. The junior dev job is disappearing AND the senior jobs have never been better. Use real examples. Show salary data if appropriate. Don't scare them -- inform them.

### Part 2: What Actually Matters (10-15 min)
Point #3. Curiosity and gumption. Tell the Apple Music story as an example of following curiosity. Tell a failure story as an example of gumption. The trail you leave (projects, open source, experiments) matters more than your resume.

### Break / Interactive (5-10 min)
Q&A, polls, or a quick activity. Let them process.

### Part 3: Playing the Long Game (10-15 min)
Point #4. College strategy. Not studying CS in college. Using it to explore, network, follow through. The relationships that shaped the career.

### Demo: "Let's Build Something Right Now" (10-15 min)
Students vote on which app to build. Then build it live using AI, in front of them.

**The three choices:**

1. **Tier List Maker** -- Pick a category (fast food, Marvel movies, school subjects), drag items into S/A/B/C/D/F tiers, share your list with friends, compare rankings. Teens are deep into tier list culture right now.

2. **Squad Quiz** -- AI generates wild questions about your squad, then exposes who actually knows you vs. who's been faking it.

3. **Song Battle Bracket** -- Submit two songs, everyone votes on which is better, winners advance bracket-style. Music is universal teen currency.

**Why this format works:**
- The vote itself is interactive -- class is participating before a line of code is written
- Building with AI demonstrates the Zapier/AI work in a tangible way
- It proves thesis point #3 live: curiosity + follow-through (picking an idea and shipping it) matters more than knowing every syntax detail
- The result is something they can actually share with friends after class
- When things break (and they will), it's a teaching moment about debugging and gumption
- Meta-layer: "I'm building what you just chose, using the tools I work on every day, right now"

**Voting app:** Live at `talk-voter.craftsman-founder.workers.dev` (CF Worker + KV). Students scan a QR code, vote on their phones, results update every 2 seconds. Source: `cf-services/api-services/talk-voter/`. Reset endpoint available between sessions.

**Tech requirements:** Laptop, internet, projector. Need to confirm with Saied what's available. Could use Claude, Cursor, or similar AI coding tool. Deploy to Vercel/Netlify for instant shareable URL.

**Prep:** Pre-test all three so any of them can be built smoothly. Have fallback screenshots/recordings if wifi dies.

### Open Q&A (remaining time)
Be ready for: "How much do you make?" "Do I need college?" "Will AI replace programmers?" "What language should I learn?"
