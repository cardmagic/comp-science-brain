# Intro to Computer Science -- Slide Flow

**Audience:** CHS Period 1, SCHS Period 4 -- first-time coders, 9th-10th graders, strong gaming interest
**Time:** ~1 hour of content, flexible within session window
**Narrative arc:** From "this is scary" to "I can do this"

---

## The Story in One Sentence

The world changed, the old path is gone, but the people who are genuinely curious -- people like you -- have never had more power.

---

## Act 1: The Hook (5 min)

### Slide 1: Opening question

"Raise your hand if you've used ChatGPT this week."

(Most hands go up.)

"Every one of you just did something that would have cost a company $200 an hour five years ago."

Pause. Let that land.

### Slide 2: Who am I?

Keep this to 60 seconds. Not a resume -- a single sentence and a few images.

- "I've been coding since I was 13. I wrote code that became Apple Music. I started a company and sold it. Today I work at Zapier teaching AI how to order pizza."
- Photo: 13-year-old Lucas's first website (magic tricks site, 1996)
- Photo: current -- working on AI at Zapier

The point: I started where you are. I was your age when this began.

### Slide 3: What this talk is about

"I'm going to tell you three things nobody told me when I was your age."

1. The honest truth about what's happening to programming jobs
2. What actually matters (it's not what you think)
3. We're going to build something together, right now, live

---

## Act 2: The Honest Truth (10 min)

### Slide 4: The junior dev job is disappearing

Don't sugarcoat this. They can handle the truth and they'll respect you for it.

- AI can now do the work companies used to hire beginners for
- The entry ramp into the industry -- writing boilerplate, fixing simple bugs, doing grunt work -- is being sawed off
- This is real and it's happening now

Visual: a ladder with the bottom rungs missing

### Slide 5: But wait

"Here's what nobody's talking about."

### Slide 6: The top has never been better

- The people who can think, architect, debug novel problems, build things that don't exist yet -- they're worth more than ever
- The gap between "can code" and "can build" has never been wider or more valuable
- General salary trajectory visual (ranges, not specifics) -- show the upward curve for senior/staff engineers

The tension: the bottom is gone, but the top is extraordinary. The question is how you get there.

### Slide 7: Transition

"So how do you actually get to the top when the bottom rungs are gone?"

---

## Act 3: What Actually Matters (15 min)

This is the heart. Four qualities, each with a story.

### Slide 8: It's not skill. It's gumption.

"AI can code. AI can't be curious. AI can't care. AI doesn't stay up until 3am because it HAS to figure out why something isn't working."

### Slide 9: Curiosity -- the 13-year-old magician

Tell the story: obsessed with magic at 13, followed a WebMonkey tutorial, built a website where magicians shared tricks. Not because someone assigned it. Because he couldn't NOT do it.

"The best projects come from combining two things you're already obsessed with. For me it was magic and the internet. What's your combination?"

Connect to their world: gaming + code = mods, game engines, custom tools. Music + code = synths, beat makers, visualizers.

### Slide 10: Follow-through -- the landing page that changed everything

Tell the story: years of building ideas nobody wanted. Then one night, too tired to code, just made an ugly landing page. Woke up to 800 signups. Two weeks later, 4,000 -- word of mouth only.

"The idea that became a $10 million company started because I was too tired to code and just shipped a landing page instead."

The lesson: follow-through doesn't mean grinding harder. It means paying attention when something catches fire.

### Slide 11: Gumption -- getting hacked by teenagers

Tell the story: company got hacked by kids roughly their age. Hadn't slept in four days. Instead of hiding it, wrote an honest blog post. 100,000+ reads. Thousands of new customers.

"When something goes wrong -- and it will -- the move is to be honest about it. That post got us more customers than any marketing we ever did."

Why this lands for Intro CS: the hackers were their age. Both sides of this story are relatable.

### Slide 12: The trail you leave

"Nobody's going to read your resume. They're going to look at what you've built."

- GitHub repos, side projects, experiments, blog posts, mods
- The trail of things you've made IS your resume
- Open source: "I gave away code for free and 9 million people downloaded it. That did more for my career than any degree."

### Slide 13: Quick pause

"Questions so far? Anything you want to push back on?"

Give them at least 15-20 seconds of silence. Don't fill it. The questions will come.

---

## Act 4: Let's Build Something (15-20 min)

### Slide 14: The vote

"Okay, you've heard me talk. Now let's do something."

QR code appears. Students scan and vote on their phones.

Three choices:
1. **Tier List Maker** -- rank anything into S/A/B/C/D/F tiers. AI roasts your bad takes and defends its own rankings.
2. **Friendship Compatibility Quiz** -- AI writes the questions based on your vibe, then scores how well your friends actually know you.
3. **Song Battle Bracket** -- songs go head-to-head, bracket-style. AI plays commentator and predicts the upset picks.

Show live results updating on the projector as votes come in.

### Slide 15: Building it live

"Whatever you just picked, I'm going to build it right now using AI."

Open Claude/Cursor. Start building. Narrate as you go:
- "I'm describing what I want in plain English."
- "Watch -- it writes the code, but I have to know if the code is RIGHT."
- "This is what my actual job looks like."

When something breaks (and it will): "This is the job. This right here. Debugging. Figuring out why it doesn't work. This is what gumption looks like."

Deploy it. Give them the URL. Let them use the thing they just watched get built.

### Slide 16: The meta-lesson

"I just built what you voted for, in front of you, using AI. Five years ago that would've taken a team a week. But I still had to know what to build, how to describe it, and how to fix it when it broke."

"That's the difference between 'can code' and 'can build.' AI handles the code. You handle the thinking."

---

## Act 5: The Close (5 min)

### Slide 17: Where I started vs. where I am

Side by side:
- Age 9: called a 1-800 number to patent a can opener
- Age 13: built a magic tricks website
- Today: staff engineer at Zapier, code used by millions, patents with Apple

"I didn't study computer science in college. I studied physics. Nobody gave me permission to do any of this. I just... couldn't stop."

### Slide 18: What I want you to remember

One slide. Three lines.

1. The junior dev job is disappearing. The senior job has never been better.
2. What gets you there is curiosity, gumption, and follow-through -- not knowledge.
3. Start now. Not in college. Now.

### Slide 19: Open Q&A

"What do you want to know? Anything. Salary, college, AI, whatever."

Be ready for:
- "How much do you make?" -- talk ranges and trajectory, not personal numbers
- "Will AI replace programmers?" -- reframe: AI replaces tasks, not curious people
- "What language should I learn?" -- the language doesn't matter, the thinking does
- "Do I need college?" -- leads into brief version of point #4: college is for exploring, networking, and follow-through, not learning to code
- Gaming questions (likely from SCHS Period 4): game dev is real engineering, modding teaches real skills, Unreal/Unity/Godot are all code

---

## Story Selection Notes

| Story | Use in Intro CS? | Notes |
|-------|-----------------|-------|
| 13-year-old magician | Yes -- centerpiece | They're this age. This is the "you can start now" proof. |
| 9-year-old inventor | Yes -- closing | Reinforces "it starts before anyone tells you to" |
| Landing page / AppFog | Yes -- simplified | Focus on the "too tired to code, shipped anyway" angle |
| Got hacked by teenagers | Yes -- big moment | Hackers were their age. Both sides are relatable. |
| Cafe rejection / network | Brief mention in Q&A only | Too abstract for beginners unless asked about it |
| Layoffs | Skip | Too heavy for intro audience |
| Quit with baby on the way | Skip | Mention only if asked about risk/fear |
| Failed ideas | Brief mention | "I failed a bunch of times first" -- don't dwell |

## Tone Reminders

- Use "you" more than "I"
- Don't say "API" without explaining it
- Don't assume they know what a startup is -- say "I started a company"
- Gaming is a legitimate entry point into CS -- validate it
- Wait at least 15 seconds after asking a question
- They can smell inauthenticity -- be real, not polished
