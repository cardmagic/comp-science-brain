# AP CS (Mixed CSP/CSA) -- Slide Flow

**Audience:** SCHS Period 5 -- AP CSP + AP CSA Java students in the same room, college-level, more advanced
**Time:** ~70 min session, ~1 hour of content. Leave room for Saied's teacher-led Q&A about college, career, entrepreneurship, scholarships.
**Narrative arc:** From "I'm on the path" to "the path is different than you think"

---

## The Story in One Sentence

You're already ahead of most people -- but the career you're imagining may not exist, and the one that does is better than you think.

---

## Act 1: The Hook (5 min)

### Slide 1: Opening question

"Raise your hand if you've used AI to help you with an assignment this year."

(Most hands.)

"Now keep your hand up if you've ever gotten AI-generated code that looked right but was completely wrong."

(Some hands stay up. Knowing laughs.)

"That gap -- between code that LOOKS right and code that IS right -- is the entire future of this industry."

### Slide 2: Who am I?

Brief. They're more advanced, so the credentials can land faster.

- Wrote the streaming code that became Apple Music (holds a patent Apple still uses)
- Founded AppFog -- raised $10M in VC, 100K+ developers, acquired
- Open source downloaded 9M+ times
- Today: staff engineer at Zapier, building AI that uses APIs in the real world
- Published programming books through O'Reilly

One key detail for this audience: "I didn't study CS in college. I studied physics."

Let that sit.

### Slide 3: Three things

"Three things I wish someone had told me when I was sitting where you are."

1. The honest truth about what's happening
2. What actually separates the people who make it
3. How to play the long game (college, career, network)

---

## Act 2: The Honest Truth (10 min)

### Slide 4: The junior dev job is disappearing

Same core message as intro, but more direct. This audience is closer to career decisions.

- Companies aren't hiring people to write boilerplate
- The entry-level ramp -- fix bugs, write CRUD, do the grunt work -- is being automated
- 3 in 5 rising seniors feel pessimistic about starting their career. They're not wrong to worry.

### Slide 5: The real picture

"But here's what the pessimism misses."

### Slide 6: The top has never been more valuable

- Senior engineers, architects, people who can debug novel problems and build systems that don't exist yet -- worth more than ever
- PRECISELY because AI handles the junior work, the people who can think are in massive demand
- Salary trajectory: general ranges showing the curve from entry to senior/staff. Not personal numbers, but enough to show the gap is enormous.

"The question isn't whether programming has a future. It's whether YOU will be the person who can do what AI can't."

### Slide 7: What AI can't do

Brief slide. Not a lecture -- a setup for the next act.

- Can't decide what to build
- Can't tell you if the code is actually correct for the problem
- Can't debug a system it doesn't understand
- Can't care about the user
- Can't have gumption

---

## Act 3: What Actually Separates People (15 min)

### Slide 8: It's not knowledge

"AI leveled the knowledge playing field. If success were about knowing syntax, ChatGPT would be a billionaire."

Four things that separate the people who make it:

### Slide 9: Serious curiosity -- the robo-advisor rabbit hole

Tell the story: after selling AppFog, read one unexplained reference to a "200-day moving average" in a Tony Robbins book. Spent months going down a rabbit hole of financial technicals. Built models. Concluded most of it was nonsense. Tried to start a fintech company around it. Failed.

"The startup failed. But the CURIOSITY -- that pattern of seeing something I don't understand and not being able to let it go -- that's the same thing that produced every success in my career."

Connect: "You're in AP CS. You chose to be here. That already says something. But are you curious about WHY Java works the way it does, or are you just trying to pass the exam?"

### Slide 10: Follow-through -- the landing page

Tell the story: years of building things nobody wanted. Online invoicing (no passion). Wedding registry (wrong customer). Seven years working at startups hoping to absorb secrets (didn't work).

Then one night: too tired to code, made an ugly landing page, posted it, went to sleep. Woke up: 800 signups. Two weeks: 4,000. Half of them filled out a detailed survey.

"Same person. Same skills. Different outcome. What changed was finding something that matched genuine curiosity with genuine demand."

"You don't get better at finding ideas. You get better at recognizing the right one."

### Slide 11: Gumption -- the layoffs

Tell the story: raised $10M, hired 40+ people, signed up 100K+ developers. Revenue didn't meet expectations. Had to do layoffs.

"I went through all five stages of grief. I tried to find workarounds. I cried. I felt like I'd let everyone down."

"But when morning came, I found deep peace. The remaining team rallied. We drove to a successful acquisition."

"Gumption isn't about never failing. It's about what you do the morning after."

### Slide 12: The trail you leave

"Your GitHub is your resume. Your side projects are your portfolio. The things you build outside of class are what get you hired."

- Open source: gave away code, 9M+ downloads, more career impact than any credential
- The hacking story (brief): got hacked, wrote an honest blog post, 100K reads, thousands of new customers. "Transparency beats polish."

### Slide 13: Check-in

"Push back. What do you disagree with? What sounds like BS?"

Give them space. This audience will have sharper questions. Don't rush past this.

---

## Act 4: Playing the Long Game (10 min)

### Slide 14: College -- the real talk

"I didn't study CS in college. And it's the best career decision I ever made."

Not "don't go to college." This is "if you go, use it deliberately."

### Slide 15: What college is actually for

Three things:

**1. Explore your curiosity broadly**
- Study philosophy, history, music, whatever pulls you
- "The best engineers I've worked with are well-rounded thinkers"
- The problems worth solving require understanding people, not just code
- "Love something besides technology"

**2. Build your network**
Tell the cafe rejection story: pitched a friend on co-founding. Friend said no before they finished their coffee. But introduced him to someone. That person introduced him to three people. Those three each introduced him to three more. That chain led to $10M in VC and an acquisition.

"One relationship leads to three, leads to nine. That's how careers actually work. Not applications. Not degrees. Introductions."

**3. Follow through on things that interest you**
- The projects and relationships you build matter more than the transcript
- Side projects > GPA
- The college experience is a four-year window to explore with low risk -- use it

### Slide 16: The courage to bet on yourself

Tell the story briefly: quit his job, highest salary ever, wife six months pregnant, sole breadwinner. Started AppFog anyway.

"The voice that says 'this is stupid, give up' never goes away. Not even after you raise $10 million. You just learn to keep going despite it."

"Courage isn't the absence of fear."

---

## Act 5: Let's Build Something (10-15 min)

### Slide 17: The vote

QR code. Students vote on their phones.

Three choices:
1. **Tier List Maker** -- rank anything. AI roasts your bad takes and defends its own rankings.
2. **Friendship Compatibility Quiz** -- AI writes the questions based on your vibe, then scores how well your friends actually know you.
3. **Song Battle Bracket** -- songs go head-to-head, bracket-style. AI plays commentator and predicts the upset picks.

Live results on projector.

### Slide 18: Build it live

Same format as intro but narrate differently for this audience:
- Show the prompting strategy: "Watch how I describe what I want. This is prompt engineering, not coding."
- When debugging: "This is where knowing how code works matters. AI wrote this, but I have to know if it's correct."
- Point out architecture decisions: "Why am I structuring it this way? Because..."

Deploy. Give them the URL. Let them use it.

### Slide 19: The meta-lesson

"I just used AI as a tool. I still had to know what to build, how to describe it, how to debug it, and how to ship it. That's the job."

"Knowing Java syntax isn't the moat. Knowing how to think about systems is."

---

## Act 6: The Close + Saied's Q&A (remaining time)

### Slide 20: What I want you to remember

1. The junior dev job is disappearing. The senior job has never been better.
2. Curiosity, gumption, and follow-through -- not knowledge -- are what separate people.
3. College is for exploring, networking, and following through. Not for learning to code.

### Slide 21: One more thing

The Vienna choir story -- brief, emotional, ties it together.

"In high school I was a choir geek. Our director was diagnosed with cancer senior year. We were supposed to go to an international competition in Vienna, but she couldn't travel. We went without her. Things went badly at first. Then we got a call that she'd taken a turn for the worse. We rallied. We practiced maniacally. We won first prize."

"We brought the trophy home to her. She passed away shortly after."

"This isn't a programming story. But it's about the same thing: courage, determination, follow-through. A group of high schoolers -- your age -- committed to something that mattered. The values that won in Vienna are the same values that matter in everything."

### Slide 22: Open to Saied + Q&A

Hand it to Saied for his questions about:
- College experience and choosing a school
- Career path: how did you decide what to work on?
- Internships: how to get them, what they're really like
- Entrepreneurship: when to start a company vs. work at one
- Scholarships: did they matter? What did?
- How you chose which areas of CS/AI to pursue

Then open it up to student questions. Be ready for:
- "How much do you make?" -- ranges and trajectory
- "Do I need college?" -- restate point #4 clearly
- "Will AI replace programmers?" -- reframe
- "What language should I learn?" -- the language doesn't matter
- "How did you get your first job?" -- the network, not applications
- "Should I do a CS degree or something else?" -- depends on your curiosity

## Story Selection Notes

| Story | Use in AP? | Notes |
|-------|-----------|-------|
| Cafe rejection / network chain | Yes -- centerpiece of college section | Perfect for "network > degree" point |
| Robo-advisor rabbit hole | Yes -- curiosity section | Shows curiosity as a pattern, not a one-time thing |
| Failed ideas (invoicing, wedding, 7 years) | Yes -- follow-through section | "Same person, same skills, different outcome" |
| Layoffs | Yes -- gumption section | Mature enough audience for this story |
| Vienna choir | Yes -- closing | Emotional anchor. Lands harder because they're the same age. |
| Quit with baby on the way | Yes -- brief, courage point | "Fear never goes away" |
| Landing page / AppFog origin | Yes -- follow-through | The "too tired to code" angle |
| Got hacked | Brief mention | Under "trail you leave" -- transparency beats polish |
| 13-year-old magician | Brief mention in intro | Don't dwell -- they're past this age |
| 9-year-old inventor | Skip | Too young for this audience to connect with |

## Key Differences from Intro CS

| Dimension | Intro CS | AP CS |
|-----------|----------|-------|
| Emotional anchor | "I started where you are" | "The path you're imagining may not exist" |
| Story weight | Age-13 origin, getting hacked | Cafe rejection, layoffs, Vienna choir |
| Demo narration | "Watch me build this" | "Watch my decision-making process" |
| College message | Brief -- "something to think about" | Detailed -- "here's how to use it strategically" |
| Closing | Empowering -- "start now" | Challenging -- "the values matter more than the skills" |
| Saied Q&A | Organic, student-driven | Structured -- Saied leads career/college questions |
| Tone | Encouraging, accessible | Respectful, direct, no hand-holding |
| Technical depth in demo | "I'm telling the AI what to build" | "Watch the architecture decisions I'm making" |
